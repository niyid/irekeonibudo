Two Android narrative RPGs, one lean engine underneath both. Ìrèké Oníbùdó sends an orphan from a shipwreck into a sea-queen's court and up a mountain toward a crown. Akara-ogun's journey through Igbo Irúnmọlẹ̀ sends a hunter's son past bush-spirits into the court of a forest king. Both are original stories built on the world and character names of D.O. Fagunwa's two founding Yorùbá novels — and both prove the same idea: that reading carefully, not swinging hardest, is what actually gets you through a story like this alive.

The Source

Two Novels That Started Yorùbá Prose Fiction

Daniel Ọlọrunfẹmi Fagunwa published Ògbójú Ọdẹ nínú Igbó Irúnmọlẹ̀ ("The Brave Hunter in the Forest of Spirits") in 1938 — the first full-length novel ever published in the Yorùbá language — and followed it in 1949 with Ìrèké Oníbùdó. Both books move through the same imaginative territory: a hunter or orphan sets out from an ordinary village, crosses into a supernatural wilderness of spirits, shape-shifters and monsters, and comes home changed by what the journey demanded of them rather than by what they defeated. Wole Soyinka's 1968 English translation of the first novel, The Forest of a Thousand Daemons, is often the doorway English readers use to reach Fagunwa at all.

These two apps are not retellings of that prose. They are original branching narratives that borrow the setting, the character names, and the moral grammar of Fagunwa's world — an orphan going down to open water, a hunter's son going into a forest of spirits — and build new, playable stories inside it. No text from either novel or its translation is reproduced anywhere in either codebase.

"The water will ask what you're worth before it lets you cross. Answer it with panic, and it keeps you."

— The Diviner, Ìrèké Oníbùdó
Architecture

One Engine, Two Worlds

Both games are separate Android projects, but they share an identical spine: Kotlin, Jetpack Compose with Material3, Room for save/continue, a single Activity driven by one StateFlow<GameState>, and a Canvas-based scene renderer that draws every illustration procedurally — no imported art, no photos, no third-party visual assets in either app. The renderer keys off a shared seven-value SceneType enum (VILLAGE, FOREST_PATH, RIVER, CAVE, SPIRIT_COURT, VICTORY, DEATH), reused across both stories but drawn differently depending on which game's palette is active — deep sea blues and ember gold for Ìrèké, forest greens and the same ember gold for Akara-ogun, so the two apps read as a family rather than a template copy-pasted twice.

Kotlin Jetpack Compose (Material3) Room persistence StateFlow, no DI framework Canvas procedural art Single Activity

Neither project reaches for Hilt or a navigation library — a single GameNavigation.kt switches screens off one Screen enum (TITLE, STORY, ENCOUNTER, INVENTORY, ENDING), and a single GameViewModel per app owns both the branching-story engine and the turn-based combat loop. It's a deliberately small surface area for what the README of each project calls being "kept intentionally lean."

Philosophy

Five Design Principles Both Games Share

📖
Principle 01

Content Is Data, Not Code

Every node, choice, creature, and charm lives as plain StoryNode/Choice/Creature values in one file. Adding a scene means adding data, never touching the engine.

🎨
Principle 02

No External Art

Every illustration is drawn at runtime by SceneCanvas from primitive shapes, gradients, and glow — original vector art, not licensed or generated imagery.

⚖️
Principle 03

Real Trade-offs, Not Flavor Text

Choices can be gated behind a minimum stat, a specific charm, or a story flag you earned earlier — meaning some doors are genuinely closed until you've paid for them.

💀
Principle 04

Death Is a Valid Ending

Every creature has a defeatNodeId that leads to a real, written ending — losing a fight is a documented outcome, not a game-over screen that discards your story.

🪶
Principle 05

Borrow the World, Invent the Plot

Setting and names come from Fagunwa's two novels; every scene, choice, and line of dialogue is original — neither game reproduces text from either book or its translation.

Case Study I
Deep Sea Blue · Ember Gold

Ìrèké Oníbùdó — From Shipwreck to Crown

You play Ìrèké, orphaned three years and owning nothing but a single cowrie shell from his late mother's waist-beads, in a town gone lawless enough that the people meant to keep order are the ones doing the stealing. A trading ship is the only visible door out — and the storm that sinks it two days later "does not behave like weather. It behaves like something looking for someone." Ìrèké goes into the water and surfaces in the sunken court of Arogidigba, queen of the deep, whose hospitality is "a hunger dressed up" as welcome. Escaping her court doesn't end the journey — it opens onto a serpent-haunted town, a flying python demanding tribute, a mountain where the sage Itanforiti tests what you've actually learned, and finally a sick princess, Ifepade, whose cure depends less on courage than on what kind of person you chose to be along the way.

Four ways to end it

Ìrèké's climax is a bedside choice, not a battle: how you propose to cure the ailing Ifepade decides whether the ending is a love story, a redeemed king, a quietly competent hero who lets the credit fall where it wants to — or, if you carried an unspent token from the spirit Adéọrun and a flag earned much earlier in the story, a fourth ending that resolves an entirely different debt. 59 story nodes lead to 7 possible endings in total, including the three ways the journey can end in death.

Case Study II
Forest Green · Ember Gold

Akara-ogun's Journey Through Igbo Irúnmọlẹ̀

Where Ìrèké begins with a shipwreck, Akara-ogun begins with a decision to leave — a hunter's son from Àyé-Oró village, following his father Alabi's trade into the Forest of Spirits, guided from the outset by the wise woman Ìmọ̀dòyè rather than stumbling into guidance the way Ìrèké does. The forest answers that guidance with escalating tests: Agbako, a shrieking spirit of sudden calamity "tall as an iroko tree"; Ẹ̀gbin, a bloated marsh-beast whose breath rots the leaves for a mile around, guarding the crossing to the spirit-king's court; and finally a Court Sentinel in beaten bronze armor, bound to test any mortal who seeks audience with King Aramada himself. The story ends not with a battle but with a question the king asks directly: why did you truly enter this forest? — and the honesty of your answer, not your combat record, is what the ending actually judges.

The question behind the question

King Aramada's final question has four possible honest answers, each gated differently: pride needs nothing, wisdom needs 55+ Ògùn, courage needs 60+ Ìgbóyà, and staying silent while holding out the bone key bypasses the question entirely for the strangest ending of the four. 41 story nodes lead to 7 possible endings, including the three ways the forest can end you instead.

Mechanics

Combat, By the Numbers

Both games run the same turn-based encounter math, tuned from a small set of named constants rather than magic numbers scattered through the code — which is also why balancing either game means editing one block, not hunting through logic:

GameViewModel.ktKotlin
companion object { private const val PLAYER_BASE_HIT_CHANCE = 45 // + igboya / 2 private const val PLAYER_CRIT_CHANCE = 12 // % chance a landed hit doubles private const val ENEMY_BASE_HIT_CHANCE = 55 // - oogun / 10 private const val ENEMY_MIN_HIT_CHANCE = 20 // floor so high OOGUN can't trivialize fights const val CHARM_OOGUN_COST = 10 }
Anatomy of a Player Turn
1
Roll to hit

Base 45% chance, plus half your current Ìgbóyà — courage genuinely raises your odds of connecting.

2
Check for a scouted weak point

If a charm like Ojú-Inú / Eye of Ìmọ̀dòyè already found a weakness this fight, every hit gets a flat damage bonus.

3
Roll for a critical

A landed hit has a 12% chance to double its damage — narrated differently each time so repeat fights don't read identically.

4
Spend Ògùn on a charm instead, if you'd rather

Most charms cost 10 Ògùn to invoke — a real resource trade-off, not a free extra action.

5
Hand control to the creature

Enemy hit chance starts at 55% and drops with your Ògùn, floored at 20% so no build ever becomes untouchable.

🐍
Fleeing has consequences, not shame

Every creature defines its own fleeNodeId alongside victoryNodeId and defeatNodeId — running from the Flying Ejò or from Ẹ̀gbin routes you into a different, fully-written branch of the story rather than just dumping you back at the last checkpoint.

Extensibility

Writing a Scene Without Touching the Engine

The whole point of keeping StoryData.kt as plain data is that adding to either game is a writing task, not an engineering one. A single node carries its own scene type, its text, and a list of choices — each of which can optionally demand a minimum stat, a specific charm, or a flag set earlier in the run:

StoryData.kt — an actual node from Ìrèké OníbùdóKotlin
StoryNode( id = "shrine_blessing", title = "The Diviner's Warning", scene = SceneType.VILLAGE, text = "...an orphan going down to open water," she says...", grantsCharm = Charm.CALMING_SAND, choices = listOf( Choice( text = "Thank her and run for the landing.", nextNodeId = "call_to_sea", effects = listOf(StatEffect(Stat.OOGUN, 5)) ) ) )
🧩
No UI changes required

Point a Choice.nextNodeId at a new node id, and the engine handles rendering, gating, and persistence automatically. This is exactly what both READMEs mean when they say extending the story is "add a node, point a choice at it, done."

Implementation Guide

For Developers: Replicating This Pattern

Because both games share the same skeleton, adapting the pattern to a third story — another Fagunwa novel, another oral epic entirely — mostly means repeating a short checklist:

1
Pick your three stats and their starting values

Both games reuse Ìgbóyà / Ògùn / Ìlera, but the model works for any three-axis system with a clear meaning for each.

2
Write your creatures before your nodes

Each Creature needs its own victory/defeat/flee node ids up front, so combat always resolves into real narrative rather than a dead end.

3
Design your palette around the setting, not the genre

Sea story got blues and coral; forest story got greens. Let the world pick the color, and keep one shared accent (gold, here) to tie sibling apps together.

4
Gate at least one ending behind something earned, not rolled

The rarest endings in both games require a specific charm plus a flag set many nodes earlier — reward attentive playthroughs, not just high stats.

Conclusion

Courage Alone Gets You Killed More Than Once

Both READMEs open with the same warning, and it isn't marketing copy — it's the actual design thesis. In both games, the character with the highest Ìgbóyà who never listens to a diviner, a turtle-spirit, or a wise woman ends up in one of the death nodes just as reliably as the coward who never leaves the village. What both stories reward, mechanically and narratively, is attention: to what a charm actually does, to what a stat gate is actually asking of you, to what a spirit-king is actually asking when he poses a question that sounds rhetorical but isn't. That's a harder thing to encode in a turn-based RPG than "hit harder" — and it's the reason two very different journeys, a shipwreck and a walk into the woods, ended up needing the exact same engine underneath them.

Both projects are open source — Kotlin, Jetpack Compose, no external art or paywalled assets.
