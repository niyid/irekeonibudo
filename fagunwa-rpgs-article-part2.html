Redrawing the Forest:
The Audit, the Repaint,
and the Wall Ahead

📅 July 2026 · 🔗 github.com/niyid/irekeonibudo · github.com/niyid/ogbojuode · 📱 Android · Kotlin · Jetpack Compose · ⏱ 11 min read

Part I described the engine both Fagunwa-world games share. This is what happened after it shipped: a full audit pass on Akara-ogun's Journey Through Igbo Irúnmọlẹ̀ that turned up dead code, theme mismatches, and combat that didn't balance the way the numbers implied — followed by a ground-up repaint of the shared Canvas renderer. It's also where we stop and ask how much further either game can honestly go before Fagunwa's own copyright, not the engine, becomes the limiting factor.

Recap

Where Part I Left Off

The first article covered two separate Android projects — Ìrèké Oníbùdó and Akara-ogun's Journey Through Igbo Irúnmọlẹ̀ — running on one shared spine: Kotlin, Jetpack Compose, Room, a single GameViewModel, and a Canvas-based SceneCanvas that drew every illustration procedurally, with no imported art in either app. That architecture hasn't changed. What changed is everything sitting on top of it, starting with a line-by-line audit of the younger of the two projects, Akara-ogun, which had shipped fast enough to accumulate the usual residue: a scene type drawn with the wrong palette, combat math that read fine in isolation but broke under certain stat combinations, and a scattering of dead code left behind by earlier refactors.

The Second Pass

What a Full Audit Actually Turns Up

None of these were dramatic bugs — the game ran, saved, and resolved every ending correctly before the audit too. What an audit pass catches is the gap between what the code intends and what it actually does, which is a quieter kind of bug and a more persistent one:

🎭
Found

Theme Mismatches

A couple of SceneType.SPIRIT_COURT nodes were still rendering with the earlier village palette from before the forest-court scenes existed — cosmetically wrong, easy to miss in a quick playtest, obvious once you're staring at 41 nodes end to end.

🧹
Found

Dead Code

An earlier combat-flow refactor had left a superseded damage-roll path unreachable but still compiling — no functional impact, but exactly the kind of thing that makes the next contributor debug the wrong function.

⚔️
Found

Combat Balance Drift

At very high Ògùn, enemy hit chance could round down far enough that certain fights became near-unlosable — the floor described in Part I's constants table didn't exist yet when this run was made; it was added as a direct result of this audit.

🐛
Found

Edge-Case Bugs

A handful of choices with stat-gate conditions were evaluated against the player's stat before an effect from the same node had applied, occasionally letting a gate pass or fail one point earlier than the story intended.

✅
Zero crashes was the bar, not the ceiling

The acceptance criterion for the pass wasn't "doesn't crash" — it was every one of the above fixed in one sweep, with the constants table you saw in Part I (including the ENEMY_MIN_HIT_CHANCE floor) as the direct output of the combat-balance findings.

Audit findings become paint
Visual Overhaul

SceneCanvas, Redrawn

The audit's cosmetic findings became the excuse for a bigger repaint. The original SceneCanvas drew each scene as flat, single-tone shapes on a solid background — functional, readable, and honestly a little flat next to the writing. The rework keeps every constraint from Part I (still no imported art, still pure Canvas primitives, still keyed off the same seven-value SceneType enum) but layers each scene with a background-to-foreground gradient wash, soft radial glow behind light sources and spirit figures, and a faint fog gradient over distant elements to fake depth on a 2D canvas.

🎨
Same constraint, more paint

Every gradient stop and glow color still comes from the same per-game palette described in Part I — sea blues and coral for Ìrèké, forest greens for Akara-ogun. The repaint adds depth to that palette; it doesn't replace it.

The Wall Ahead

Why This Doesn't Become a Full Animation-Adventure Game

Every repaint pass raises the same question: what if these became full animated adventure games — sprite animation, cutscenes, voice, the works? The honest answer is that copyright, not engineering effort, is the ceiling on how far either project can go.

D.O. Fagunwa died on 7 December 1963. Under Nigeria's Copyright Act, literary works are protected for the life of the author plus 70 years — which puts Ògbójú Ọdẹ nínú Igbó Irúnmọlẹ̀ (1938) and Ìrèké Oníbùdó (1949) under copyright in Nigeria into the early 2030s. Wole Soyinka's 1968 English translation, The Forest of a Thousand Daemons, carries its own separate copyright as a distinct translated work, independent of the original Yorùbá text's term. Neither book is in the public domain yet, in Nigeria or in most jurisdictions with a similar life-plus-70 rule.

⚖️
Not legal advice — just the reasoning behind a design choice

This isn't a legal opinion, and copyright questions like fair dealing, transformative use, and estate permissions are genuinely fact-specific. What follows is simply why both projects have stayed conservative rather than an assessment of what would or wouldn't be permitted.

Both apps already sit deliberately close to the safe end of that line: they reuse Fagunwa's world and character names — public facts about published books, not protected expression — while every scene, line of dialogue, and story branch is original writing, with no text lifted from either novel or Soyinka's translation. That's a comfortable place to stand. Turning either app into something closer to a full animated adaptation — cutscenes built around specific published scenes, character designs matched to known illustrated editions, marketing that leans on the books' titles and cover art — moves away from "inspired by the world" and toward "derivative of the specific work," which is exactly the territory copyright law is built to gate until the term expires or an estate grants rights. Until Fagunwa's Yorùbá originals age into the public domain, or a license is arranged with his estate, both games are staying exactly where Part I described them: original interactive fiction borrowing a setting, not adaptations of the novels themselves.

Staying inside the line — and looking at what's still possible inside it
Next Iteration

What We're Testing Next: libGDX and the Bench Beyond Canvas

None of that copyright ceiling stops the engine itself from getting more ambitious inside the bounds both games already respect — richer motion, particle effects for charm invocations, parallax scene transitions. Jetpack Compose's Canvas can do a lot of that, but it starts fighting the framework once you want true sprite-sheet animation, a scene graph, or a particle system rather than hand-rolled gradients. The next iteration is evaluating a small set of alternatives before committing to one:

libGDX (Java/Kotlin, LibGDX scene2d + particle editor) KorGE (Kotlin-native, multiplatform) Godot + Kotlin/Android bindings Compose Canvas, extended (status quo)
🕹️
Candidate

libGDX

Mature, battle-tested 2D engine with a real scene graph, sprite-sheet animation, and a built-in particle editor for charm effects. Biggest lift to integrate into an existing Compose app — it wants its own GLSurfaceView-style render target — but the ceiling on what's animatable is much higher.

🧵
Candidate

KorGE

Kotlin-native and multiplatform like the rest of the Techducat stack, with a lighter footprint than libGDX. Smaller community and fewer battle-tested examples, but a more natural fit for a codebase that's deliberately avoided Java-first tooling everywhere else.

🌐
Candidate

Godot + Kotlin bindings

The most full-featured option — a real editor, animation timeline, and shader support — at the cost of the biggest architectural detour from "one Kotlin/Compose Android app" toward "an embedded game engine with an Android host."

🖌️
Baseline

Compose Canvas, extended

Zero migration cost and everything documented in this article already runs on it. The likely outcome is that Canvas stays the renderer for narrative scenes, with libGDX or KorGE tried specifically for combat-encounter animation, where a real sprite/particle pipeline earns its complexity.

🧪
Evaluation, not a rewrite commitment

Nothing here replaces the current architecture yet. The plan is a small, isolated spike — one combat encounter re-rendered in libGDX behind a feature flag — before any decision about adopting it across either game.

Conclusion

The Repaint Was the Easy Part

Fixing a palette mismatch, deleting dead code, and adding a hit-chance floor are the kind of changes that show up cleanly in a diff. Deciding how far a piece of original interactive fiction can lean on a still-copyrighted novel's world before it stops being original is the harder, less code-shaped problem — and it's the one that actually sets the boundary on what "Fagunwa RPG, v3" is allowed to look like. Everything else — layered gradients, a real particle system, a libGDX-powered combat scene — is just craft, and craft is the part that's still fully in our hands.

Both projects remain open source — Kotlin, Jetpack Compose, no external art or paywalled assets.
